Designing automated factories to not require humans on-site
## Real-world examples
### <span id=".22Lights_out.22_computer_numerical_control_.28CNC.29_machining"></span>"Lights out" computer numerical control (CNC) machining
### <span id="Existing_.22lights-out_factories.22"></span>Existing "lights-out factories"
In the manufacturing of [integrated circuits](/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit") using 300 mm wafers, the entire [manufacturing process](/wiki/Manufacturing "Manufacturing") is completely automated<sup>\[*[<span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2022)">citation needed</span>](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]</sup>, with workers only making sure that the process runs without problems and repairing any faulty machinery.<sup>\[*[<span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2022)">citation needed</span>](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]</sup>.
## Motivations for lights-out factories
Lights-out manufacturing may increase productivity and lower upkeep costs. Companies incorporating lights-out methodologies into floor plans only need to consider robotic workers, which minimize space and climate-control requirements. Human laborers can be dispatched to a separate location for tasks such as quality assurance. Optimizing manufacturing space for a fully autonomous robotic workforce allows for an increase in productivity.<sup>\[*[<span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2020)">citation needed</span>](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]</sup>

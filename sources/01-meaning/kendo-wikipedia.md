## Concept and purpose
### Concept
Kendo is a way to discipline the human character through the application of the principles of the *[katana](/wiki/Katana "Katana")*.
### Purpose
To cultivate a vigorous spirit
And through correct and structured training,
To strive for improvement in the art of Kendo.
To hold in esteem courtesy and honor.
To associate with others with sincerity.
And to forever pursue the cultivation of oneself.
Thus will one be able:
To love one's country and society;
To contribute to the development of culture;
And to promote peace and prosperity among all people.

## Modern practice
Kendo training is quite noisy in comparison to some other martial arts or sports. This is because *kendōka* use a shout, or <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*[kiai](/wiki/Kiai "Kiai")*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">気合い</span></span>)</span>, to express their fighting spirit when striking. Additionally, *kendōka* execute <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*fumikomi-ashi*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">踏み込み足</span></span>)</span>, an action similar to a stamp of the front foot, during a strike.
Kendo techniques comprise both strikes and thrusts. Strikes are only made towards specified target areas<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">打突-部位</span></span>, <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*datotsu-bui*</span><span style="margin-left:.09em">)</span></span> on the wrists, head, or body, all of which are protected by armor. The targets are *men*, *sayu-men* or *Yoko-men* (upper, left or right side of the *men*), the right *kote* at any time, the left *kote* when it is in a raised position, and the left or right side of the *dō*. Thrusts<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">突き</span></span>, <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*tsuki*</span><span style="margin-left:.09em">)</span></span> are only allowed to the throat. However, since an incorrectly performed thrust could cause serious injury to the opponent's neck, thrusting techniques in free practice and competition are often restricted to senior *dan* graded *kendōka*.
  *Kendōka* perform *sonkyo* after combat.
  Two *kendōka* in *tsuba zeriai*
  *Kendo* target areas, or *datotsu-bui*
  Two *kendōka*, one (left) is playing in *nitō* (two sword style) and the other (right) is playing in *ittō* (one sword style).
Once a *kendōka* begins practice in armor, a practice session may include any or all of the following types of practice:
[Kirikaeshi](/wiki/Kirikaeshi "Kirikaeshi") <span style="font-weight: normal">(<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">切り返し</span></span>)</span>  
Striking the left and right *men* target points in succession; practicing centering, distance, and correct technique while building spirit and stamina.
*Waza-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">技稽古</span></span>)</span>  
*Waza* or technique practice in which the student learns and refines the techniques of kendo with a receiving partner.
*Kakari-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">掛稽古</span></span>)</span>  
Short, intense, attack practice which teaches continuous alertness and readiness to attack, as well as building spirit and stamina.
*Ji-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">地稽古</span></span>)</span>  
Undirected practice where the *kendōka* tries all that has been learned during practice against an opponent.
*Gokaku-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">互角稽古</span></span>)</span>  
Practice between two *kendōka* of similar skill level.
*Hikitate-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">引立稽古</span></span>)</span>  
Practice where a senior *kendōka* guides a junior through practice.
*Shiai-geiko*<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">試合稽古</span></span>)</span>  
Competition practice which may also be judged.
## Techniques
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb">
</figure>
### Shikake-waza
These attack techniques are used to create an opening in an opponent by initiating an attack, or striking boldly when the opponent has created an opening. Such techniques include:
*Tobikomi-waza* (飛び込み技)
This is a technique used when one's opponent has weak *kisei* (spirit, vigor) or when they yield an opening under pressure. Always hold *kisei* and strike quickly.
*Hikibana-waza* (引き鼻技)
Body and *shinai* will lose balance as the initiator strikes or when being attacked. This technique takes advantage of this to help execute a strike. A good example is *Hikibana-kote* when a strike is made to an opponent's *kote* as they feel threatened and raise their *kensen* as the initiator pushes forward.
*Katsugi-waza* (担ぎ技)
This provides a surprise attack by lifting the *shinai* over the initiator's shoulder before striking. Here a skillful use of the *kensen* and spirited attack is crucial for effective *katsugi-waza* or luring the opponent into breaking their posture.
*Nidan-waza* (二段技)
There are two types. The first is for moving to the next *waza* after a failed first strike, and the second holds the opponent's attention and posture to create the opening for a second strike. The former requires a continuous rhythm of correct strikes. The latter requires continuous execution of *waza*, to take advantage of the opponent's opening.
*Harai-waza* (払い技)
This can be used if one's opponent's stance has no opening when the opponent tries to attack. The opponent's *shinai* is either knocked down from above or swept up from below with a resulting strike just when their stance is broken.
*Debana-waza* (出鼻技)
This technique involves striking the opponent as they are about to strike. This is because their concentration will be on striking and their posture will have no flexibility to respond. Thus *debana-waza* is ideal. This can be to any part of the opponent's body, with valid strikes being: *debana-men*, *debana-kote*, and *debana-Tsuki*.
### <span id=".C5.8Cji-waza"></span>Ōji-waza
These counter-attack techniques are performed by executing a strike after responding or avoiding an attempted strike by the opponent. This can also be achieved by inducing the opponent to attack, then employing one of the *Ōji-waza*.
*Nuki-waza* (抜き技)
Avoiding an attack from another, then instantly responding. Here, timing has to be correct. A response that is too slow or fast may not be effective. Therefore, close attention to an opponent's every move is required.
*Suriage-waza* (刷り上げ技)
If struck by an opponent's *shinai*, this technique sweeps up their *shinai* in a rising-slide motion, with the right (*ura*) or left (*omote*) side of the *shinai*. Then strike in the direction of their *shinai*, or at the opening resulting from their composure's collapse. This technique needs to be smooth. That is, do not separate the rising-slide motion and the upward-sweeping motion or it will not be successful. Valid strikes include: *men-suriage-men*, *kote-suriage-men*, *men-suriage-do*, *kote-suriage-kote*, and *Tsuki-suriage-men*.
*Uchiotoshi-waza* (撃落し技)
This *waza* knocks an opponent's *shinai* to the right or left. This neutralises a potential strike and gives the ideal chance to strike as an opponent is off-balance. For success, the distance between oneself and the opponent has to be correctly perceived, and then one knocks down their *shinai* before their arm fully extends. Valid strikes include: *do-uchiotoshi-men* and *Tsuki-uchiotoshi-men*.
*Kaeshi-waza* (返し技)
This technique is a response. As the opponent strikes, the opponent parries their *shinai* with the initiator's. They then flip over (turn over the hands) and strike their opposite side. Valid strikes include: *men-kaeshi-men*, *men-kaeshi-kote*, *men-kaeshi-do*, *kote-kaeshi-men*, *kote-kaeshi-kote*, and *do-kaeshi-men*.
## Rules of competition
</figure>
- *Men-bu*, the top or sides of the head protector (*sho-men* and *sayu-men*).
- *Kote-bu*, a padded area of the right or left wrist protector (*migi-kote* and *hidari-kote*).
- *Do-bu*, an area of the right or left side of the armour that protects the torso (*migi-do* and *hidari-do*).
- *Tsuki-bu*, an area of the head protector in front of the throat (*Tsuki-dare*).
<span title="Hepburn transliteration">*Zanshin*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">残心</span></span>)</span>, or continuation of awareness, must be present and shown throughout the execution of the strike and the *kendōka* must be mentally and physically ready to attack again.
In competition, there are usually three referees<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">審判</span></span>, <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*shinpan*</span>)</span>. Each referee holds a red flag and a white flag in opposing hands. To award a point, a referee raises the flag corresponding to the color of the ribbon worn by the scoring competitor. Usually, at least two referees must agree for a point to be awarded. Play is stopped after each point is awarded.
Kendo competitions are usually a three-point match. The first competitor to score two points, therefore, wins the match. If the time limit is reached and only one competitor has a point, that competitor wins.
In the case of a tie, there are several options:
- <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*Hiki-wake*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">引き分け</span></span>)</span>: The match is declared a draw.
- <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*Enchō*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">延長</span></span>)</span>: The match is continued until either competitor scores a point.

## Advancement
### Grades
In Japan, kyu ranks are generally held by children. The exam for 1st kyu (ikkyū) is often their first exam and grade. Adults generally will do their 1st dan (shodan) as their first exam.<sup>\[*[<span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2022)">citation needed</span>](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*\]</sup> In most other countries outside of Japan, kendoka go through every kyu rank before being eligible for dan ranks.

### Titles
Titles<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">称号</span></span>, <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*shōgō*</span>)</span> can be earned in addition to the above *dan* grades by *kendōka* of a defined *dan* grade. These are <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*renshi*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">錬士</span></span>)</span>, <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*kyōshi*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">教士</span></span>)</span>, and <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*hanshi*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">範士</span></span>)</span>. The title is affixed to the front of the *dan* grade when said, for example <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*renshi roku-dan*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">錬士六段</span></span>)</span>. The qualifications for each title are below.

| <span title="Hepburn transliteration">*kyōshi*</span><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">教士</span></span>)</span> | *renshi* 7-*dan* | After receiving 7-*dan*, one must wait 2 or more years, pass screening by the kendo organization, and receive a recommendation from the regional organization president, then pass an exam on kendo theory. |
